On the Matter of Recording

For much of its remembered life, Longspooning has resisted all efforts at formal record.

The sport has been kept by firelight, by witness, and by the unreliable memory of those who stood, drank, argued and endured.

After prolonged resistance, and not without objection, the Keepers of the Copse have agreed that a public record may be kept, provided it does not presume to outrank the Field, nor to settle that which is properly disputed in person.

This agreement is not universal. Some Orders have refused outright, maintaining that no true account can exist beyond the witness of those present.

The Association does not compel them.

It records what it can. It omits what it must. It is challenged where it should be.

Where the written word fails, the Field remains. Where the Field is divided, Eccles remembers.
