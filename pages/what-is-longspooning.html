What is Longspooning?

Longspooning is a competitive woodland sport in which a longspooner must take a chosen morsel by mouth from the end of a long wooden shaft, balanced upon the shoulder, without touching the morsel by hand, biting the spoon, or losing the confidence of the Field.

It is played at night, by firelight where possible, in the presence of those who stand and see.

The Aim

To take the morsel.

This has proven, over time, to be more difficult than it first appears.

The Method

Each longspooner bears a longspoon — a length of wood, shaped at one end into a flat or gently concave spoon. The shaft rests upon the shoulder, aided only by a pauldron of natural fibre offering honest friction.

Hands may guide the shaft. They may not claim the morsel.

The tooth may meet the spoon. It may not command it.

The Field

Longspooning is not played alone.

A gathering forms — competitors, shaftsmen, elders, and those who have come simply to see.

Those who come and see are the witnesses.

The Field judges all things: whether a morsel was taken or lost; whether tooth became bite; whether a pauldron is honest; whether a shaftsman was true; whether a longspooner wandered, cheated, or disgraced himself.

There is no appeal beyond the Field. Disapproval is enforcement.

The Shaftsman

Each longspooner may be advised by a shaftsman. The shaftsman may guide, warn, mock or steady by word alone. He may not touch the longspooner, the shaft, or the morsel during a live attempt.

A shaftsman must be true.

The Morsel

If it can be consumed, and is deemed worthy of challenge, it may be a morsel. The morsel rests upon the spoon end. It is not fixed in place.

Roundness is treachery. Bravado drives difficulty upward.

A Final Word

Longspooning appears simple to those who have not attempted it.

It is not.
