A Short and Unreliable History of Longspooning

The history of Longspooning is agreed by none, recorded by few, and argued by many.

On the First Bringing Ashore

It is commonly claimed that Longspooning was first brought ashore by Hrolf Shoulder-Bound, who is said to have carried the practice from the sea to land.

A rival tradition insists that Hrolf’s shaft was insufficient in length to constitute true Longspooning, and that the first worthy bringing ashore was instead the work of Ivar Broadshaft.

The dispute is not settled.

The Hidden Orders

Following the supposed bringing ashore, Longspooning is said to have spread inland slowly and without agreement. Travel was limited. Gatherings were local. Customs diverged.

Fragments from the Flagon

The earliest written record of Longspooning is said to be a document known as Fragments from the Flagon, believed to date from not long after the Norman Conquest. No verified copy has been produced.

The Salted Reach

Among the most frequently cited early Orders is the Order of the Salted Reach, said to have gathered along the northern coast. No reliable record survives. Attempts have been made in later years to restore its supposed shore play. None have gained lasting acceptance.

Eccles Copse

At some point, not precisely known, Eccles Copse became the place most closely associated with the highest level of Longspooning. Eccles does not explain itself.

The Modern Recording

After prolonged resistance, the Keepers of the Copse permitted the establishment of a public Archive, on the condition that it would not claim authority over the Field.

It is incomplete. It is contested. It is, in places, wrong. It is also, at present, the best that can be done.
